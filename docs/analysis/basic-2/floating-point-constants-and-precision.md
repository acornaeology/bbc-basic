# The constant that isn't quite *e*: BBC BASIC's 5-byte floats and the precision of its constant pool

> **Scope.** This article describes **BBC BASIC II** — the 16 KB language ROM of the BBC Micro — and the packed 5-byte floating-point format it uses for stored REAL constants. The format and the byte-level figures are specific to this version (BASIC IV is the same format at relocated addresses; BASIC V on ARM uses IEEE doubles). Addresses are version II.

Tucked into the maths section of the ROM is a pool of pre-computed REAL constants — *e*, π/2, ln 2, log₁₀*e*, the degree/radian conversion factors, and the coefficient tables for the trig and log series. Disassembling them raised a small but stubborn question: when the ROM says it holds *e*, does it?

It does not. It holds a 32-bit approximation that **agrees with *e* to nine significant figures and then diverges** — and a close look at the whole pool shows the last-bit rounding isn't even consistent from one constant to the next. This article works through the format, the evidence, and why the disassembly deliberately shows the full decoded value rather than a tidy rounded one.


## The 5-byte format

A packed BBC BASIC REAL is five bytes:

```
  +0      exponent   (excess-128; 0 means the whole value is zero)
  +1..+4  significand (32 bits, big-endian)
```

The significand is a normalised fraction in `[0.5, 1)`, so its top bit (bit 31, the MSB of byte +1) is *always* 1. Rather than waste it, the format **overwrites that always-1 bit with the sign** and treats the leading 1 as implied. To decode, restore it:

```
  sign = byte1 & 0x80                       ; 1 = negative
  S    = ((byte1 | 0x80) << 24) | (byte2 << 16) | (byte3 << 8) | byte4
  value = ±S × 2^(exponent − 160)
```

(The `−160` folds together the `−128` exponent bias and the `2^−32` that turns the 32-bit integer `S` back into a fraction.)

That 32-bit significand is the whole story for precision: 32 bits is `32 × log₁₀2 ≈ 9.63` decimal digits. **Every REAL the BBC Micro ever computes carries about nine-and-a-half significant figures and no more.** The constants in the ROM can be no better than that, and — as we'll see — are individually a fraction of a unit-in-the-last-place worse.


## *e*, byte by byte

The constant at [`&AAE4`](address:AAE4@2?hex) is five bytes:

```asm
.flt_e
    equb &82, &2d, &f8, &54, &58    ; bbc_float5 = 2.718281827867031  e
```

Decode it: exponent `&82` = 130; significand `(0x2d|0x80),f8,54,58` = `0xADF85458` = `2918732888`. So the stored value is exactly the rational

```
  2918732888 / 2^30  =  2.718281827867031
```

Set that beside the true constant:

| | value |
|---|---|
| ROM constant at `&AAE4` | `2.718281827867031` |
| true *e* | `2.718281828459045` |
| difference | `5.92 × 10⁻¹⁰` |

They agree through `2.71828182`, then part company at the tenth digit (`…7` vs `…8`).

**Why it can't be exact.** *e* is irrational, so no finite binary fraction equals it; the format can only hold the nearest representable value. One ULP at this magnitude is `2^(130−160) = 2^−30 ≈ 9.31 × 10⁻¹⁰`, and the shortfall is about `0.64` ULP — exactly the kind of error you get from reducing *e* to 32 significant bits.

**And it isn't even the *best* 32-bit value.** The exact significand is `e × 2³⁰ = 2918732888.64`. Rounded to nearest that is `2918732889`; the ROM stores `2918732888` — the floor. For *e*, Acorn **truncated** rather than rounded to nearest, leaving the stored value one ULP low.


## Not a uniform policy

The obvious next hypothesis — "these tables were all truncated" — turns out to be wrong. Decoding the six named scalars and comparing each stored significand against the exact `value × 2^(160−exp)` gives:

| constant | addr | exp | ROM significand | exact significand | last bit is | error |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| *e* | `&AAE4` | 130 | 2918732888 | 2918732888.64 | **truncated** (1 below nearest) | −0.64 ULP |
| π/2 | `&AA63` | 129 | 3373259426 | 3373259426.13 | floor = nearest | −0.13 ULP |
| ln 2 | `&A86E` | 128 | 2977044472 | 2977044471.82 | **rounded** to nearest | +0.18 ULP |
| log₁₀*e* | `&A869` | 127 | 3730561194 | 3730561193.22 | **one ULP above** nearest | +0.79 ULP |
| π/180 | `&AA68` | 123 | 2398762258 | 2398762258.58 | **truncated** (1 below nearest) | −0.58 ULP |
| 180/π | `&AA6D` | 134 | 3845054675 | 3845054675.12 | floor = nearest | −0.12 ULP |

Of the constants where truncation and round-to-nearest *differ* (the fractional part isn't tiny), the behaviour is mixed: *e* and π/180 are truncated, ln 2 is correctly rounded, and **log₁₀*e* sits a full ULP above the correctly-rounded value** — its stored value is high, not low, so it can't be a truncation at all.

So the pool was not generated by one clean rule. Each constant is within about a ULP of the truth — perfectly serviceable for nine-figure arithmetic — but whoever produced the table didn't apply a single consistent round-to-nearest discipline. (`log₁₀e` being a ULP high is consistent with its having been formed as `1 / ln 10` from a slightly-low `ln 10`, then not corrected.) The honest summary is: **good to ~9 figures, last bit idiosyncratic.**


## Why the disassembly shows the full value

This is precisely why the typed-data annotation emits the whole decoded figure and not a rounded one:

```asm
    equb &82, &2d, &f8, &54, &58    ; bbc_float5 = 2.718281827867031  e
```

Printed as `2.718281828`, the constant would read as exact *e* and hide the truncation entirely. Anyone reasoning about the ROM — or wondering why BASIC's `EXP`, `LN` or `DEG`/`RAD` disagree with a pocket calculator in the last couple of digits — would be misled into thinking the inputs were perfect and the routines lossy. They aren't: the *constants themselves* are already a fraction of a ULP off, before a single multiply happens. The full repr makes the stored reality visible; the comment after it (`e`) names the *intent* without claiming the bytes achieve it.


## Working past the limit: the two-part π/2

Acorn knew 32 bits wasn't always enough, and one corner of the pool shows them engineering around it. Argument reduction for `SIN`/`COS` needs `x − n·(π/2)` accurately even when `n` is large, and a single 32-bit π/2 loses low-order bits in the product. So π/2 is stored **twice**:

- [`&AA59`](address:AA59@2?hex): a deliberately *low-precision* high part, `−1.57080078125`, whose significand `&C9100000` has its bottom two bytes zeroed — so `n × C₁` is computed without rounding error;
- [`&AA5E`](address:AA5E@2?hex): the correction `4.454455 × 10⁻⁶`, holding the bits the high part dropped.

Their sum is `−π/2` to far better than 32 bits, and the reduction subtracts the two parts in turn. It's a textbook Cody–Waite split, and a neat acknowledgement, in the ROM itself, of the very precision ceiling this article is about. (Both halves are typed `bbc_float5` in the disassembly, so their decoded values sit right beside the bytes.)


## In the disassembly

All 39 packed constants in the pool — the named scalars, the `1.0`/`−0.5` terms, and every coefficient of the ATN/SIN/EXP/LN continued-fraction tables — are marked with the `bbc_float5` typed-data type (dasmos 1.10.0). The five raw bytes are still emitted, so the image reassembles byte-identical, while the decoded value rides along as an annotation (and as a structured `decoded` field in the JSON). That is what makes a claim like "the *e* in the ROM is `2918732888 / 2³⁰`, not *e*" something you can read straight off the listing rather than having to work out by hand.
